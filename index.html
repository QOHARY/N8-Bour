N8-Bour

This website is dedicated to eight potentially habitable exoplanets located within 100 light-years of our solar system, offering some of the most exciting prospects for future exploration. These nearby worlds, positioned in the habitable zones of their stars, hold the promise of liquid water and the right conditions for life. As we delve into their mysteries, you’ll learn about their diverse environments, geological features, and the cutting-edge technology that allows us to study them. Join us in imagining the possibilities of human exploration and the rich discoveries waiting to be made on these intriguing celestial neighbors.

Proxima Centauri b

Proxima b, an Earth-size planet right outside our solar system in the habitable zone of its star, may not be able to keep a grip on its atmosphere, leaving the surface exposed to harmful stellar radiation and reducing its potential for habitability.
GJ-1061d

possibly habitable worlds in tight orbits around small, red-dwarf stars in our galaxy. GJ 1061d is part of a system of at least three planets a mere 12 light-years from Earth. Its surface temperature might be just right for liquid water.
ROSS-128b

Important discovery from Europe reveals a likely terrestrial, Earth-mass planet only 11 light-years away. Its now the second-nearest known terrestrial planet to Earth. the planet orbit in the inner edge of its star habitable zone, where there could be water.

TRAPPIST-1e

TRAPPIST-1e is roughly Earth-sized, yet has around 0.7 times the mass of our planet. It is the fourth planet from its star, orbiting at just 0.028 times the distance between Earth and the sun, completing one orbit in just 6.1 Earth days.
Wolf-1061c

“It is a particularly exciting find because all three planets are of low enough mass to be potentially rocky and have a solid surface, and the middle planet, Wolf 1061c, sits within the ‘Goldilocks’ zone where it might be possible for liquid water – and maybe even life — to exist,” says lead study author UNSW’s Dr Duncan Wright.
Teegarden's Star b

One of the two new planets that were detected orbiting Teegarden's Star, an ultracool, red dwarf less than 13 light-years away. At a minimum, the new planets both weigh in at about 1.1 times the mass of Earth
LHS-1140b

The plannet's orbit in the habitable zone of a small, dim star called LHS 1140 that lies in the constellation Cetus. The exoplanet was discovered in 2017 and has been observed by multiple telescopes since.
TRAPPIST-1f

TRAPPIST-1f, are far enough from the host star that water could be frozen as ice across these surfaces. If they have thin atmospheres, they would be unlikely to contain the heavy molecules of Earth, such as carbon dioxide.
